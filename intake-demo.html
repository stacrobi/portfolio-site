AI-Enabled Learning System

Learning request intake that scopes the work before the build begins.

This demo captures the business problem, audience, desired performance, constraints, and source materials, then turns that information into a first-pass instructional design recommendation. It is designed to reduce vague requests, speed up triage, and create clearer conversations with sponsors and SMEs at project kickoff.

What this demo is doing

The form behaves like a structured intake conversation with an instructional designer. As fields are completed, the panel on the right converts raw request data into a draft scope, likely delivery path, and design risks.

  • 1. Capture the real business need Prompt the requester to describe the performance issue, not just ask for training.
  • 2. Surface readiness and constraints Highlight missing metrics, limited source content, and timeline pressure before design starts.
  • 3. Generate a first-pass recommendation Suggest whether the work points toward training, performance support, or deeper analysis.
Requester Information

Identify the requester and the person accountable for the business outcome so the work stays tied to an actual sponsor, not just a generic training ask.

Business Need

Describe the performance issue in business language. The goal is to understand what is breaking, who it affects, and what would happen if nothing changed.

Best answer: a performance or workflow problem, not just "we need training."
Learner Audience

Clarify who needs to perform differently. Audience shape determines whether the solution should scale, stay lightweight, or be piloted first.

Desired Outcomes

Define the observable behavior or capability that should improve. The cleaner this gets, the better the eventual design and measurement strategy will be.

Delivery and Constraints

Record the requester's preferences, but do not assume the preferred delivery is automatically the right answer. Constraints often matter more than format preference.

SMEs and Existing Materials

Good source content lowers build risk. Weak source content means the project may need more discovery, more review cycles, or a narrower MVP approach.
